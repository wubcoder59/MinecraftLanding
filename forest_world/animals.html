Forest Wildlife

Discover the diverse animals that call our forests home

Our Forest Ecosystem

Forest World is home to a rich diversity of wildlife, from tiny insects to large mammals. Our protected areas provide habitat for over 250 animal species, many of which are threatened or endangered. As you explore our trails and observation areas, you'll have the opportunity to witness these remarkable creatures in their natural environment.

Our wildlife programs emphasize ethical observation practices that minimize disturbance to animals. By maintaining appropriate distances and using designated viewing areas, we ensure that wildlife can continue their natural behaviors while visitors enjoy meaningful encounters.

Mammals

Eastern Gray Squirrel 🔊

Sciurus carolinensis Year-round resident
Least Concern

The Eastern Gray Squirrel is one of our most visible mammals. These agile tree-dwellers can be spotted gathering nuts and seeds throughout the forest, especially in the oak and hickory sections. They play a crucial role in forest regeneration by burying nuts and seeds, many of which grow into new trees.

Did You Know?

Squirrels can find food they've buried months later, even under a foot of snow, thanks to their excellent spatial memory and sense of smell.

White-tailed Deer 🔊

Odocoileus virginianus Year-round resident
Least Concern

White-tailed deer are graceful browsers that can be spotted in forest clearings, especially during dawn and dusk. Males grow new antlers each year, which are shed in winter. These adaptable mammals have a significant influence on forest understory composition through their selective feeding habits.

Did You Know?

Deer can jump up to 10 feet high and 30 feet in length in a single bound, allowing them to easily clear fences and obstacles when evading predators.

American Black Bear 🔊

Ursus americanus Year-round resident
Least Concern

The American Black Bear is the largest predator in our forest. Despite their name, these bears can be brown or cinnamon colored as well as black. They are omnivorous, feeding primarily on berries, nuts, and insects, though they occasionally take small mammals. Bears are most active in early morning and evening.

Did You Know?

Black bears have an incredible sense of smell that is approximately seven times more sensitive than a bloodhound's, allowing them to detect food from over a mile away.

Birds

Northern Cardinal 🔊

Cardinalis cardinalis Year-round resident
Least Concern

The striking red plumage of the male Northern Cardinal makes it one of our most recognizable birds. These vocal songbirds maintain territories year-round and can often be heard singing their clear, whistled phrases from prominent perches in forest edges and thickets.

Did You Know?

Unlike many songbirds, female cardinals also sing. Mated pairs often vocalize back and forth to each other, and the female may sing to communicate nest location to her mate.

Pileated Woodpecker 🔊

Dryocopus pileatus Year-round resident
Least Concern

The crow-sized Pileated Woodpecker is our largest woodpecker species. With their striking red crest and powerful bill, these birds excavate distinctive rectangular holes in dead trees as they search for their favorite food: carpenter ants. Their loud drumming and cackling calls echo through the mature forest sections.

Did You Know?

The abandoned nest cavities of Pileated Woodpeckers provide essential shelter for many forest species, including owls, ducks, bats, and pine martens.

Wood Thrush 🔊

Hylocichla mustelina Summer breeding
Vulnerable

The Wood Thrush is renowned for its beautiful, flute-like song that resonates through the forest at dawn and dusk. These medium-sized songbirds nest in the understory of mature deciduous forests and feed primarily on soil invertebrates. They are neotropical migrants, spending winters in Central America.

Did You Know?

The Wood Thrush can sing two notes simultaneously through its Y-shaped voice box, allowing it to harmonize with itself during its ethereal songs.

Reptiles & Amphibians

Eastern Box Turtle 🔊

Terrapene carolina Year-round resident
Vulnerable

The Eastern Box Turtle is a terrestrial turtle with a high-domed shell that can be closed completely to protect the animal from predators. These slow-moving reptiles can live for 100 years or more and maintain small home ranges throughout their lives. Look for them in moist forest floors and along woodland edges.

Did You Know?

Box turtles can remember the locations of feeding areas for many years, demonstrating remarkable spatial memory despite their small brain size.

Red-spotted Newt 🔊

Notophthalmus viridescens Year-round resident
Least Concern

The Red-spotted Newt has one of the most complex life cycles of any forest species. It begins life as an aquatic larva, transforms into a bright orange juvenile (red eft) that lives on the forest floor for 1-3 years, then returns to water as an olive-green adult. These striking amphibians can be found in our forest pools and moist woodland areas.

Did You Know?

Red efts produce a powerful neurotoxin in their skin that makes them unpalatable to most predators. Their bright orange coloration serves as a warning.

Timber Rattlesnake 🔊

Crotalus horridus Active April-October
Endangered

The Timber Rattlesnake is a venomous pit viper that plays an important role in controlling rodent populations. These secretive reptiles prefer remote, rocky outcrops and mature forests with minimal human disturbance. Despite their fearsome reputation, they are not aggressive and will use their rattle as a warning when disturbed.

Did You Know?

Timber rattlesnakes add a new segment to their rattle each time they shed their skin, which happens several times per year. However, the segments can break off, so rattle size is not a reliable indicator of age.

Wildlife Watching Tips

Observing wildlife is one of the most rewarding experiences at Forest World. Follow these guidelines to maximize your chances of meaningful wildlife encounters while respecting animal welfare.

🕗

Timing Is Everything

Visit during dawn and dusk when many forest animals are most active. These "golden hours" are when mammals feed and birds are particularly vocal.

👔

Dress Appropriately

Wear neutral colors that blend with the environment. Avoid bright clothing, perfumes, or scented products that might alert wildlife to your presence.

🤫

Move Slowly & Quietly

Walk softly, speak in whispers, and avoid sudden movements. Patience is key—sometimes the best strategy is to find a comfortable spot and wait.

🔭

Bring Binoculars

Quality optics allow you to observe animals from a distance that doesn't disturb their natural behavior. Our visitor center offers binocular rentals.
